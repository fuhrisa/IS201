Basic Chord Structure and Intervals

Learning basic chord structure and intervals is fundamental to understand music theory and how to play music. Here's a quick summary:

  • Chord Structure:
    • Chords are formed by stacking notes in intervals, typically in thirds.
    • Basic chords consist of a root note, a third (major or minor), and a fifth.
    • Major chords have a major third interval between the root and third, while minor chords have a minor third interval.
  • Intervals:
    • Intervals are the distance between two pitches.
    • They are named based on their distance in the musical scale (e.g., major second, perfect fifth).
    • Understanding intervals helps in building chords, recognizing melodies, and improvising.
  • Common Chord Types:
    • Major chords: Root, major third, perfect fifth.
    • Minor chords: Root, minor third, perfect fifth.
    • Seventh chords: Add a seventh interval on top of basic chords, creating richer harmonic textures.
    • Other extensions and alterations can be added to create more complex chords.

12-Bar Blues Form

The 12-bar blues follows a specific chord progression that spans 12 measures or bars. Each bar typically contains four beats. The most common 12-bar blues progression is the I-IV-V progression, where the chords are built on the first (I), fourth (IV), and fifth (V) degrees of the scale. For example, in the key of E, the chord progression would be E7 - A7 - B7.


Scales

Two important scales to learn include the pentatonic scales and the blues scale.

  • Pentatonic Scale
    • Five-Note Scale: Pentatonic scales consist of five notes per octave, in contrast to the seven-note diatonic scale. The term "penta" means five, hence the name pentatonic.
    • Major and Minor: There are two primary types of pentatonic scales: major pentatonic and minor pentatonic. Major pentatonic scales are built from the major scale, while minor pentatonic scales derive from the natural minor scale.
    • 5 Positions: There are five pentatonic positions on the guitar fretboard, accounting for 5 different chord shapes - C, A, G, E, and D. These positions can be transposed over the entire fretboard depending on what key the song is.

(Major Pentatonic Shapes)


  • Blues Scale
    • The blues scale typically consists of five to six notes per octave, depending on whether the "blue note" is included. It is a variation of the pentatonic scale, with the addition of the flattened fifth, known as the "blue note."
    • The minor blues scale consists of the root, minor third, perfect fourth, diminished fifth (blue note), perfect fifth, and minor seventh. The major blues scale includes the root, major second, minor third, major third (blue note), perfect fifth, and major sixth.
